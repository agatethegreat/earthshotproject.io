Adaptive Traffic Signal Control

Making Cities Flow

Cutting-edge computer vision and adaptive control to eliminate congestion at the intersections existing systems can't handle.

The Problem Scale

Current Default (Fixed Timing)
  • An engineer programs a strict cycle: green for 45s, yellow for 3s, red for 30s.
  • Runs the exact same cycle at 3:00 AM with no cars as it does during 5:00 PM rush hour.
  • No updates or highly inconsistent changes to timing over years.
The Solution (Adaptive Timing)
  • System measures what is actually at the intersection right now.
  • Extends green if there is a long queue detected.
  • Cuts green short if the road is completely empty.
  • Adjusts in real-time, every single cycle.

Our Primary Target Context

Current market opportunities focus almost exclusively on classifying cars in clean, grid-like systems. We are building for the complex, messy realities of urban waterfronts and irregular corridors.

🎯 Intersection #1 Pilot: 169 Lake Shore Blvd W

This is exactly the type of intersection where incumbent systems break down. Located at Lake Shore Blvd W + Rees St + Lower Simcoe St, it features:

  • Irregular geometry: Not a clean 4-way intersection.
  • Adverse lighting: Gardiner Expressway overhead creates challenging shadows and artificial light mixes.
  • Heavy mixed-use: Merging traffic in the middle of the intersection.
  • Massive foot traffic: Tourists heading to the CN Tower and Roundhouse Park, mixed with commuters.
  • Active transit: Cyclists on the waterfront trail and multiple visible crosswalks.

The Perception Layer

The perception layer our team is building—detection with tracking and pose estimation—is exactly what production systems are beginning to use. We are closing the gap on the part everyone admits is still weak: pedestrian-aware, irregular-intersection deployment.

Computer Vision Detection is Production-Ready
  • Improved YOLOv8 models for synchronous vehicle and pedestrian detection achieve 95.23% mAP at 41.46 FPS in complex urban scenes.
  • YOLO-APD reaches 77.7% mAP@0.5:0.95 with >96% pedestrian recall at 100 FPS.
  • CARLA-SUMO co-simulation work confirms that YOLOv5-based MARL (Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning) traffic signal control significantly improves flow even with imperfect detection.

The takeaway: Our perception stack does not need to be 100% perfect to win and improve flow.

Sources: Synchronous YOLOv8 (NIH) | YOLO-APD | CARLA-SUMO MARL

Honest Weaknesses (And How We Address Them)

We are building a defensible product by acknowledging and engineering around the known limitations of current tech:

Transparent Technical Challenges
  • Low-Light & Occlusion: Computer-vision-only systems struggle in adverse weather and shadows (which is why Pittsburgh uses radar+camera fusion). We are engineering specifically for the Gardiner Expressway's overhead light mix.
  • Downstream Coordination: The ScienceDirect State College study found that ATSC at all intersections doesn't always help. Coordination with downstream intersections matters deeply, meaning a single-intersection demo is not the whole story.
  • Well-Funded Incumbents: Vendors like Surtrac, NoTraffic, Miovision, and Yunex have deep pockets. Our differentiator has to be genuine—pedestrian-first design and irregular geometry—not just "we also do adaptive signals."

The Competitive Landscape

The adaptive signal control market has well-funded incumbents, including Surtrac (Rapid Flow Technologies), NoTraffic, Miovision, and Yunex Traffic. However, their primary deployments are vehicle-centric.

The Positioning Wedge

Our positioning is not "cheaper Surtrac." Our positioning is "Surtrac for the intersections Surtrac can't handle."

Lake Shore Blvd + Rees + Lower Simcoe is literally the failure mode of existing systems. Surtrac's creators themselves admitted during their deployments that they "got feedback from pedestrians who were feeling left out." That specific gap—pedestrian-aware, irregular waterfront intersections—is our wedge.

Cost is Not the Bottleneck

Cities have the money to solve this. What they lack is a system that handles weird geometry and pedestrian-heavy waterfront intersections gracefully.

  • Surtrac costs ~$20K per intersection to wire up.
  • Despite this cost, studies (like the Florida DOT evaluation) show a Benefit-Cost Ratio (BCR) of 5.4 to 12.8.
  • Because the ROI is so definitively proven, municipal budgets are not the primary barrier—deployment capability in complex zones is.

Proof That It Works

The case for adaptive signal control is no longer speculative. It is a 13-year-old, government-validated, peer-reviewed result. We have a clean evidence ladder.

Fact 1: Pittsburgh's Surtrac Pilot (The Flagship)
The original 9-intersection deployment at Baum Boulevard in 2012 produced a 25% reduction in travel time, 40% reduction in vehicle wait time, 30–31% reduction in stops, 41% reduction in idling, and a 21% reduction in vehicle emissions, all benchmarked against existing actuated signal timing.
Sources: Carnegie Mellon Robotics Institute | Smart Cities Dive
Fact 2: Florida DOT 8-Corridor Study (Multi-City Validation)
Adaptive Signal Control Technology cut travel time by an average of 9.36% across eight Florida cities, with a benefit-cost ratio of 5.4 (operational benefits only) to 12.8 (including safety). One of the most rigorous independent evaluations on record.
Source: U.S. DOT ITS Knowledge Resources
Fact 3: Nature Communications 2025 (The Climate Stakes)
Big-data adaptive signal control in China's 100 most congested cities reduced peak-hour trip times by 11%. Deploying at just the first 20% of intersections in Shanghai cut peak times by 8%. Estimated annual benefit: 31.73 million tonnes of CO₂ reduction and US$31.82 billion in societal benefits against a US$1.48 billion cost.
Source: Nature Communications
Fact 4: FHWA Meta-Finding (The Regulatory Baseline)
The U.S. Federal Highway Administration concludes that adaptive signal control improves average performance metrics by 10% or more, with improvements of 50%+ in systems with outdated timing. Crashes can be reduced by up to 15%. This is the conservative baseline we anchor to.
Source: FHWA Adaptive Signal Control Technologies
📈 Meta-Analysis: The Numbers Cluster Tightly

The numbers converge across independent studies. Pittsburgh (~25%), Bellevue WA (~36–43%), Toronto MARLIN simulation (15–26%), Florida (9.36%), and China at scale (8–11%). Different cities, methodologies, and vendors all converge on double-digit percentage travel time reductions. We aren't relying on one cherry-picked study.
